ENTREXT – 72 Hour Build Assignment 
Entrext is a venture studio. We build small and large software products that people and 
businesses actually enjoy using. We care about choosing the right problems, moving fast, and 
building things that truly work. This assignment is designed to show us how you think and how 
you act when time is limited and things are not clearly defined. 
You have seventy two hours to complete this assignment. The goal is simple: take one real 
problem that many people struggle with and turn it into a working software feature. Not a design, 
not an idea, not a presentation. Something real that a user can actually use. 
You must choose one problem from the following list.  
_________________________________________________________________________ 
The Problems (Choose ONE) 
Niche - Outcome Based Learning Platforms 
Pick only one of the problems below. 
These are real problems, asked again and again by many people online . 
1. Intellectual Community Fragmentation 
Problem Statement: Curious individuals lack small, structured, high-quality spaces for 
intellectual discussion outside of formal education. 
2. Intellectual Discovery Fatigue 
Problem Statement: In an era of infinite content, discovery feels overwhelming rather 
than enriching. Users struggle to find meaningful, high-quality intellectual experiences. 
3.  Student Cognitive Load Overload 
Problem Statement: Students struggle not due to workload quantity, but due to poor 
cognitive load distribution across time. 
4. The Silent Learning Gap Problem 
Problem Statement: I finished many online courses, but I still can’t build anything on              
my own. 
5. The Curiosity-to-Action Gap 
Problem Statement: Many learners consume inspiring content daily but rarely convert 
curiosity into structured action or skill-building. 
You don’t need to prove the problem exists. 
Your job is to pick one and build something useful for it. 
______________________________________________________________________ 
Within seventy two hours, you are expected to  
Choose one problem, clearly understand who the user is, decide on one useful feature, 
and build that feature completely. This includes what the user sees and the logic behind 
it that makes it work. You are allowed to use any tools you want, including AI, no-code 
tools, low-code tools, APIs, templates, or automations. The focus is on speed and 
usefulness, not on how the product is built.  
There are a few important rules. You only have seventy two hours. You must build only one 
feature. You should not think about a full product or future roadmap. Designs or mockups alone 
are not enough. The feature must actually work. If a real user cannot use it, it does not count. 
At the start, spend time truly understanding the problem you picked. Write a short 
explanation describing who the user is, when this problem feels the worst for them, what they 
have already tried that did not work, and why this situation is frustrating or stressful. Keep this 
simple and honest. Fancy language is not needed. 
Next, decide what single feature you will build. This feature should solve one clear 
moment of the problem, not the entire problem. Be very clear about what the feature does 
and what it does not do. Think about what changes for the user after using it once. If you 
cannot explain the feature in very simple words, it is probably too big.Research about the 
competitor and try to make the feature which causes a differentiation. 
After that, build and ship. You should create a simple interface that a user can interact with and 
connect it to real logic behind the scenes. Data should be saved, processed, or generated in a 
meaningful way. The user should clearly see a result or benefit after using it. Login systems, 
payments, and analytics are optional and should only be added if they are truly required for the 
feature to work. 
( You can take a look at an application called Secret Room[secretroom.entrext.in] / CurioCafe    
[curiocafe.entrext.com] , it's a great example of building one feature based application ) 
At the end, reflect on your work. Explain why you chose this problem over the others. Share 
what assumption you are most unsure about. Talk about what surprised you while building. Be 
honest about whether you would personally use this product. Finally, explain how this feature 
could grow into a small paid product and how it might later become part of a bigger business. 
We are not judging perfect design, advanced technology, or big market theories. We are looking 
at how you choose a problem, how clearly you think, how well you control scope, how fast you 
move, and whether you can turn an idea into something real under pressure. This assignment 
helps us understand whether you think like a builder and a founder.
